16/08/2016 Consumer Price Inflation (CPI), July 2016 Summarises the movements in prices of the items in the designated basket of goods in the year to July. Covers:
  • CPI inflation rate
 Period immediately after referendum CPI inflation is the rate at which in the prices of goods and services bought by households rise or fall. 2% CPI inflation tells us that £100 a year ago is worth about £98 today. Movements will reflect both the degree of strength of domestic demand but also changes in the UK prices of imports. The sharp depreciation of the Pound makes the Sterling price of imports more expensive may be a key factor in coming months 13/09/2016 for August 2016 estimates
16/08/2016 Producer Prices (PPI), July 2016 Summarises the movements in input and output prices of producers in the production sector in the year to July. Covers:
  • Input indices and growth rates
  • Output indices and growth rates
 Period immediately after referendum Input and output prices both reflect the changing costs for UK manufacturers. They include but not limited to costs of materials and fuels bought by producers as well as labour cost, energy cost and rent. Changes in the output PPI inflation rate could be due to changes in labour and energy cost. The depreciation of Sterling, to the extent it causes imported inputs to be more expensive could have an upward pressure on input prices, and ultimately output prices. 13/09/2016 for August estimates
16/08/2016 House Price Index (HPI), June 2016 Summarises movement in house prices in the year to June. Note that these are still experimental statistics. Covers:
  • House price index
  • Annual change in house prices
 Majority of period was before the referendum In the short term, house prices are an important indicator of household confidence and activity in the housing market. Slower or declining house prices could mean that households are putting off decisions to buy houses. In the longer term, decisions to buy houses may change as households reassess the economic and their financial situations post referendum. 13/09/2016 for July estimates
17/08/2016 Labour Market Statistics , mostly representing averages for the three month period April to June 2016. Summarises the state of the UK labour market in the 3 months to June and comprises:· April-June 2016 estimates for:
  •  Employment
  • Unemployment
  • Average weekly earnings;
· July 2016 estimates for the claimant count; and · May-July 2016 estimates for vacancies. Also included will be estimates of employment by country of birth and nationality (April-June 2016).		 The monthly Claimant Count estimates will relate to July 2016 which was after the referendum. The remainder of the estimates to be published in the release will mainly be for the April to June period. The health of the labour market is important because most people in the UK rely on a job as a source of income. In general, the labour market does better in periods of economic growth and vice versa in periods of recession and uncertainty. GDP growth in Q2 2016 shows that the economic backdrop to the labour market was generally positive in the period leading up to polling day.   Published labour market data for March-May show the labour market continued to strengthen in this period.  This period overlaps by two months with the period for April to June covered in this release. 14/09/2016 for mostly averages of the three month period May to July 2016
